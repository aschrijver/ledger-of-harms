Center for Humane Technology

Ledger of Harms

Alpha Version Updated August 27, 2018

Technology and social media companies have benefited our lives enormously. But even with the best intentions, companies are under pressure from shareholders to prioritize product usage and growth, which can create invisible harms in society.

This Ledger collects those negative impacts of technology that do not show up on the balance sheets of companies, but on the balance sheet of society. We evaluate the information we receive before we publish it here, including a methodological review of all the statistical studies we include. The data below comes from peer-reviewed academic studies, as well as non-peer reviewed studies; academic conference proceedings; industry sources; and books and journalism from widely trusted public outlets.

This is a work in progress. We hope it will guide future research and coverage of these issues, and we welcome your feedback on how to improve it.

The Ledger of Harms is prepared by the Center for Humane Technology in partnership with Omidyar Network, the J.W. Couch Foundation, and Common Sense.

Attention

Loss of ability to focus without distraction

Introduction

Attention and cognition are the foundation on which all our capacities depend — our ability to think, to concentrate, to solve problems, and be present with each other. Technology's constant interruptions and precisely-targeted distractions, which have been designed to keep us more engaged with tech products, are taking a major toll on these critical functions.

Attention

Evidence

The presence of a smartphone, even when off, can reduce cognitive capacity by taxing the attentional resources that reside at the core of both working memory capacity and fluid intelligence

Copy Link Peer-Reviewed · Ward, Duke, Gneezy, and Bos, 2017. Journal of the Association for Consumer Research ↗
72%

of teens and 48% of parents feel the need to immediately respond to texts, social-networking messages, and other notifications

Copy Link Non Peer-Reviewed · Lake Research Partners, 2016. Common Sense Media ↗

In a study of 15 mobile device users, half of notifications were viewed by users within a few minutes of their arrival, and email + mobile messenger apps interrupted users in almost 50% of cases

Copy Link Conference Proceedings · Pielot, Church, and de Oliveira, 2014. MobileHCI ↗

Almost 90% of 290 undergraduates reported feeling "phantom vibrations," which were experienced an average of once every two weeks

Copy Link Peer-Reviewed · Drouin, Kaiser, and Miller, 2012. Computers in Human Behavior ↗

44% of teens agree at least “somewhat” that using social media often distracts them from people they're with in person, and 34% agree either strongly or somewhat that using social media takes away from time they could be spending with people face-to-face

Copy Link Non Peer-Reviewed · Rideout and Saphir, 2012. Common Sense Media ↗

Commercial vehicle drivers who text message while driving are 23.2x more likely to be involved in a "safety-critical event"

Copy Link Non Peer-Reviewed · Olson, Hanowski, Hickman, and Bocanegra, 2009. U.S. Dept of Transportation ↗

Mental Health

Loneliness, depression, stress, loss of sleep, and even increased risk of suicide

Introduction

Although social media can help people find support when they’re lonely or isolated, it also constructs a distorted social reality that challenges our mental wellbeing. Some evidence shows that when people use social media a lot, they’re more likely to be isolated, stressed, and depressed. Photo-sharing products emphasize the highlight reels of our friend’s lives, not their inner challenges and insecurities, and it’s never been easier to see 24/7 evidence of our friends having fun without us.

Mental Health

Evidence

A systematic review and meta-analysis (of 20 studies) showed strong, consistent evidence of an association between bedtime access to or use of devices and reduced sleep quantity and quality, as well as increased daytime sleepiness

Copy Link Peer-Reviewed · Carter, Rees, and Hale, 2016. Journal of the American Medical Association: Pediatrics ↗
86%

of American adults report that they "constantly or often" check email, texts, and social media; for these "constant checkers," the average reported overall stress level is 5.3 out of 10, compared with 4.4 for those who don’t check as frequently

Copy Link Non Peer-Reviewed · Harris Poll, 2017. American Psychological Association ↗

44% of teens agree at least “somewhat” that using social media often distracts them from people they're with in person, and 34% agree either strongly or somewhat that using social media takes away from time they could be spending with people face-to-face

Copy Link Non Peer-Reviewed · Rideout and Saphir, 2012. Common Sense Media ↗

Reading on a blue-lit screen iPad in the hours before bed, versus reading the same book on paper, correlated with a 90-minute shift in circadian rhythms and suppresses levels of the sleep-promoting hormone melatonin. [Note: Given the small sample size and modest effect sizes, this study is best considered in conjunction with other similar studies]

Copy Link Peer-Reviewed · Chang, Aeschbach, Duffy, and Czeisler, 2015. Proceedings of the National Academy of Scientists ↗

Electronic device use is weakly correlated with teen depression and suicide, and social media use is weakly correlated with depressive symptoms, especially for girls

Copy Link Book · Twenge (citing the Youth Risk Behavior Surveillance Survey administered by the CDC), 2017. iGen ↗

50% of teens and 27% of parents feel "addicted" to their mobile devices

Copy Link Non Peer-Reviewed · Lake Research Partners, 2016. Common Sense Media ↗

Relationships

Less empathy, more confusion and misinterpretation

Introduction

Technology has created amazing tools for long-distance correspondence. But conversations that are mediated and interrupted by technology create less emotional connection and carry higher risk of misinterpretation. Since tech companies get better engagement metrics for online conversations than offline conversations, they are incentivized to pull people towards digital conversation — and what if that replaces in-person connections?

Relationships

Evidence

Even the mere presence of smartphone can disrupt the connection between two people, having negative effects on closeness, connection, and conversation quality

Copy Link Peer-Reviewed · Przybyliski and Weinstein, 2013. Journal of Social and Personal Relationships ↗

People overestimate their ability to correctly interpret sarcasm, humor, or sincerity over text communication, and this means people tend to believe they can communicate over e-mail more effectively than they actually can

Copy Link Peer-Reviewed · Kruger, Epley, Parker, and Ng, 2005. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology ↗

Digital communications miss out on advantages of in-person conversation, including body language, voice, tone, and silences

Copy Link Book · Turkle, 2015. Reclaiming Conversation ↗

In a survey of web-savvy users, many reported that the Facebook news feed was confusing or violated their expectations, sometimes in ways that harmed their relationships (as for example when a user doesn't see a post from a close friend who expected them to see it), and indicated frustration at how Facebook fails to inform them of how its feed surfaces information

Copy Link Conference Proceedings · Rader and Gray, 2015. Proceedings of the 33rd Annual ACM Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems ↗

Democracy

Propaganda, lies, an unreliable and noisy space to talk

Introduction

Platforms like Facebook, Twitter, and Reddit have built a remarkable new public square. Yet these platforms can be confusing at the very times when people most need solid information, because tech platforms don't have a clear business reason to evaluate truth. Platforms can be irresponsible about how their policies or product changes affect entire nations, especially when they don't have any employees or loved ones in those nations. And platforms rarely take responsibility for protecting users against trolls and malicious agents, because taking responsibility is expensive and complicated — even if the platform's product design encourages bad behavior.

Democracy

Evidence

18.5%

of total tweets from the top 50k active users on Twitter came from bots, when sampled during three weeks of the 2016 presidential election

Copy Link Peer-Reviewed · Bessi and Ferrara, 2016. First Monday ↗

Facebook's product team is out of touch with many countries, such as Cambodia in 2017, where they tested a news feed feature change that drastically shrank the audience for activist writers during political unrest

Copy Link Journalism · Larson, 2017. Foreign Policy ↗

Search results can shift the voting preferences of undecided voters by 20% or more, with virtually no one aware they are being manipulated

Copy Link Peer-Reviewed · Epstein and Robertson, 2015. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences ↗

50,258 Russian-linked Twitter accounts tweeted election-related content during the 2016 election period

Copy Link Industry Source · Twitter Public Policy Team, 2018. Twitter ↗

150 million Americans are estimated by Facebook to have been reached by Russian propaganda in the 2016 election

Copy Link Industry Source · Ackerman quoting Facebook general counsel Colin Stretch, 2017. The Daily Beast ↗

129 events were created by Russian propaganda groups on Facebook, and 60,000 people indicated they were attending those events

Copy Link Industry Source · Facebook general counsel Colin Stretch, 2018. US Senate Select Committee on Intelligence ↗

Many Facebook users are so confused by how Facebook's news feed works that they come up with "folk theories" — or even conspiracy theories — to explain how it works

Copy Link Conference Proceedings · Eslami, Karahalios, Sandvig, Vaccaro, Rickman, Hamilton, and Kirlik, 2016. Proceedings of the 2016 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems ↗

5,000 - 10,000 protesters actually attended, in person, and marched in a protest that was organized on Facebook by a Russian propaganda account

Copy Link Journalism · Breland, 2017. The Hill (this protest was also documented by The Guardian) ↗

Children

Children face new challenges learning and socializing

Introduction

Kids' cognitive challenges with learning, as well as social challenges such as bullying and body image issues, may be exacerbated by the tech they use. Parents can help by taking an active role in talking to their kids about tech, but parents are often struggling to understand and manage similar tech problems themselves.

Children

Evidence

Children who are cyberbullied are 3x more likely to engage in suicidal ideation than non-bullied children, while those who experience "traditional" bullying are 2x more likely to engage in suicidal ideation.

Copy Link Peer-Reviewed · van Geel, Vedder, and Tanilon, 2014. Journal of the American Medical Association: Pediatrics ↗

78% of teens check their devices at least hourly, and 50% report feeling “addicted” to their phones; meanwhile, 69% of parents check their devices at least hourly, and 27% of parents feel "addicted"

Copy Link Non Peer-Reviewed · Lake Research Partners, 2016. Common Sense Media ↗

86% of over 2,200 teachers said the number of students with social challenges has increased in the 3-5 years before 2015; 90% said they saw increased emotional challenges, and 77% cognitive challenges

Copy Link Non Peer-Reviewed · McRae, Rich, Varhagen, Daniels, and Bickham, 2015. Growing Up Digital Alberta ↗

Parents increasingly report horrifying videos slipping past YouTube filters to be viewed by their children

Copy Link Journalism · Maheshwari, 2017. The New York Times ↗

Disturbing videos targeted at kids racked up tens of billions views before YouTube removed them in a crackdown in November 2017

Copy Link Journalism · Gutelle, 2017. TubeFilter ↗

67% of over 2,200 teachers say the number of students who are distracted by digital technologies in the classroom is growing; 76% of teachers say that their students’ ability to focus on educational tasks decreased in the 3 to 5 years before 2015

Copy Link Non Peer-Reviewed · McRae, Rich, Varhagen, Daniels, and Bickham, 2015. Growing Up Digital Alberta ↗

Toy Freaks, a YouTube channel that specialized in gross-out situations and posted videos of children vomiting, had 8.53 million subscribers and was one of YouTube's top 100 most-viewed channels before YouTube shut it down

Copy Link Journalism · Spangler, 2017. Variety ↗
